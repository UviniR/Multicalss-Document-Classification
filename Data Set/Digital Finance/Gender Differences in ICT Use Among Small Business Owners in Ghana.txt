ABSTRACT
In﻿the﻿last﻿two﻿decades,﻿ microfinance﻿ institutions﻿ across﻿ regions﻿ have﻿ helped﻿ to﻿financially﻿ build﻿
small﻿ businesses﻿ in﻿areas﻿ of﻿disadvantaged﻿ populations.﻿ The﻿efforts﻿ in﻿providing﻿ entrepreneurs﻿ with﻿
small ﻿non-collateralized ﻿loans (﻿or﻿micro-loans) ﻿along ﻿with﻿developing ﻿cooperative ﻿programs ﻿for﻿
entrepreneurs﻿ were﻿ reported﻿ in﻿earlier﻿ studies﻿ as﻿being﻿ helpful﻿ in﻿alleviating﻿ some﻿ borrowers﻿ from﻿
extreme﻿ economic﻿ poverty.﻿ However,﻿ early﻿ warning﻿ signals﻿ were﻿ raised﻿ about﻿ whether﻿ microfinance﻿
institutions﻿ were﻿ benefiting﻿ themselves﻿ more﻿ than﻿ the﻿poor.﻿ This﻿ article﻿ assesses﻿ the﻿differences﻿ in﻿
attitudes ﻿toward ﻿the﻿use﻿of﻿information ﻿and﻿communication ﻿technology ﻿among ﻿small ﻿business ﻿owners﻿
in﻿Ghana ﻿that﻿have﻿access ﻿to﻿micro-loans. ﻿The﻿findings ﻿indicate ﻿significant ﻿differences ﻿between ﻿interest﻿
payers﻿ and﻿interest﻿ non-payers﻿ based﻿ on﻿region﻿ of﻿the﻿country,﻿ age,﻿education,﻿ and﻿membership﻿ in﻿
an﻿entrepreneurship﻿program.

21. INTRODUCTION
In﻿this﻿decade,﻿ writers﻿ are﻿asking﻿ questions﻿ like﻿the﻿following﻿ about﻿ the﻿viability﻿ of﻿micro-loans﻿ and﻿
credit:
1.﻿﻿Is﻿microfinance﻿really﻿for﻿the﻿poor?
2.﻿﻿How﻿ can﻿micro﻿ and﻿small﻿ enterprises﻿ in﻿Sub-Saharan﻿ Africa﻿ become﻿ productive(﻿ Mano,﻿ 2012)?
3.﻿﻿Does﻿microfinance﻿still﻿hold﻿promise﻿for﻿reaching﻿the﻿poor(﻿Cull,﻿2015)?In﻿a﻿World﻿ Bank﻿ extensive﻿ field﻿ research﻿ study﻿ on﻿whether﻿ Microfinance﻿ still﻿held﻿ promise﻿ in﻿
reaching﻿the﻿poor,﻿the﻿findings﻿as﻿reported﻿by﻿Cull(﻿2015)﻿were:•﻿Micro-loans﻿had﻿modest﻿benefits
•﻿Commercial﻿ microfinance﻿ was﻿not﻿likely﻿ to﻿reach﻿ those﻿ that﻿were﻿ described﻿ as﻿the“﻿poorest﻿ of﻿
the﻿poor”
•﻿Other﻿ forms﻿ of﻿microfinance﻿ like﻿micro-savings,﻿ micro-insurance﻿ may﻿help﻿to﻿provide﻿ financial﻿
inclusion
•﻿The﻿alternative﻿ delivery﻿ methods﻿ and﻿channels﻿ like﻿the﻿use﻿of﻿information﻿ and﻿communication﻿
technology(﻿ ICT)﻿ for﻿microfinance﻿ delivery﻿ held﻿promise﻿ for﻿the﻿poor﻿ who﻿had﻿access﻿ and﻿could﻿
facilitate﻿faster﻿financial﻿inclusionThis﻿ paper﻿ looks﻿ at﻿small﻿ businesses﻿ with﻿ micro-loans﻿ and﻿the﻿use﻿of﻿ICT﻿as﻿a﻿delivery﻿ method.﻿
The﻿use﻿of﻿ICT﻿for﻿microfinance﻿ services﻿ is﻿relatively﻿ new﻿in﻿many﻿ countries﻿ of﻿Sub-Saharan﻿ Africa﻿
including ﻿Ghana. ﻿In﻿the﻿study ﻿that﻿is﻿reported, ﻿the﻿authors ﻿looked ﻿at﻿understanding ﻿significant﻿
differences ﻿in﻿the﻿use﻿of﻿ICT﻿by﻿interest ﻿payers ﻿and﻿interest ﻿non-payers ﻿of﻿micro-loans ﻿among﻿
entrepreneurs﻿in﻿the﻿Greater﻿Accra,﻿Central,﻿Eastern﻿and﻿Western﻿regions﻿of﻿Ghana.
2. LITERATURE REVIE w
The﻿reach ﻿out﻿to﻿disadvantaged ﻿communities ﻿by﻿Grameen ﻿Bank’s ﻿micro-loan ﻿initiatives ﻿in﻿Bangladesh,﻿
the﻿ACCION’s﻿ BancoSol﻿ in﻿Boliva﻿ and﻿several﻿ others﻿ made﻿ microfinance﻿ institutions﻿ in﻿the﻿late﻿
1980s﻿ and﻿1990s﻿ to﻿be﻿seen﻿ as﻿doing﻿ something﻿ very﻿ important﻿ to﻿raise﻿ hope﻿ and﻿end﻿generational﻿
and﻿extreme﻿poverty(﻿Bhatt﻿&﻿Tang,﻿2001,﻿Smith﻿and﻿Yunus,﻿2007;﻿Sullivan,﻿2007;﻿Yunus,﻿1998).
2.1. Microfinance in Ghana
By﻿2005, ﻿Sub﻿Saharan ﻿African ﻿countries, ﻿particularly ﻿Ghana ﻿had﻿witnessed ﻿the﻿influx ﻿of﻿many﻿
non-indigenous﻿ microfinance﻿ institutions﻿ as﻿well﻿as﻿local﻿ startups.﻿ The﻿reports﻿ at﻿the﻿time﻿ were﻿ that﻿
extreme﻿ poverty﻿ was﻿being﻿ gradually﻿ reduced﻿ through﻿ micro-loans﻿ and﻿development﻿ of﻿new﻿models﻿
of﻿rural﻿ entrepreneurship(﻿ Afrane,﻿ 2002;﻿ Anthony﻿ et﻿al,﻿2011;﻿ Aryeetey,﻿ 1992,﻿ 1998﻿ and﻿2005).﻿ The﻿
Country ﻿had﻿both﻿well-formed ﻿private ﻿sector, ﻿small ﻿to﻿medium ﻿enterprises (﻿SMEs) ﻿and﻿governmental﻿
microfinance﻿ institutions(﻿ MFIs)﻿ like﻿rural﻿ banks﻿ that﻿focused﻿ on﻿providing﻿ micro-loans.﻿ Donkor﻿ and﻿
Duah (﻿2013), ﻿reported ﻿on﻿the﻿relationships ﻿between ﻿the﻿savings ﻿and﻿credit ﻿among ﻿rural﻿banks ﻿in﻿Ghana.﻿
The﻿first﻿rural﻿bank﻿was﻿founded ﻿in﻿1976. ﻿Since ﻿then,﻿a﻿total﻿of﻿126﻿rural﻿banks ﻿and﻿counting ﻿have﻿been﻿
established ﻿in﻿Ghana ﻿with﻿good ﻿distribution ﻿among ﻿the﻿regions ﻿as﻿follows: ﻿Greater ﻿Accra (﻿6),﻿Ashanti﻿
(23),﻿Eastern (﻿22),﻿Volta (﻿14),﻿Central (﻿22),﻿Brong ﻿Ahafo (﻿18),﻿Western (﻿14),﻿Upper ﻿East(﻿2),﻿Northern﻿
(3)﻿and﻿Upper﻿ West(﻿ 2).﻿It﻿is﻿worth﻿ noting﻿ here﻿that﻿due﻿to﻿resource﻿ limitation,﻿ the﻿study﻿ reported﻿ in﻿
this﻿paper ﻿was﻿conducted ﻿in﻿only﻿four﻿regions ﻿of﻿Ghana: ﻿Greater ﻿Accra, ﻿Central, ﻿Eastern ﻿and﻿Western.﻿
The﻿Bank﻿ of﻿Ghana(﻿ the﻿Central﻿ Bank﻿ of﻿the﻿Country)﻿ and﻿several﻿ governmental﻿ agencies﻿ including﻿
the﻿Ministry﻿ of﻿Private﻿ Sector﻿ Development﻿ and﻿the﻿National﻿ Board﻿ for﻿Small﻿ Scale﻿ Industries﻿ had﻿International Journal of ICT Research in Africa and the Middle East
Volume 8 • Issue 1 • January-June 2019
3direct﻿ oversight﻿ of﻿the﻿SME﻿ private-sector﻿ lenders﻿ and﻿formulated﻿ policy﻿ to﻿aid﻿their﻿ growth.﻿ One﻿
of﻿the﻿identifiable﻿ challenges﻿ in﻿the﻿early﻿ part﻿of﻿the﻿decade﻿ that﻿was﻿facing﻿ Ghana’s﻿ microfinance﻿
sector,﻿ according﻿ to﻿Annim(﻿ 2011),﻿ was﻿not﻿the﻿lack﻿of﻿SMEs﻿ or﻿MFIs,﻿ rather,﻿ it﻿was﻿establishing﻿ the﻿
regulatory﻿ provisions﻿ that﻿could﻿ control﻿ the﻿wide﻿ differential﻿ in﻿interest﻿ rate﻿charges﻿ for﻿micro-loans.﻿
Annim(﻿ 2011)﻿ further﻿ argued﻿ that﻿the﻿Bank﻿ of﻿Ghana﻿ needed﻿ to﻿step﻿up﻿and﻿institutionalize﻿ Apex﻿
banks﻿ and﻿help﻿ to﻿regulate﻿ Ghana’s﻿ Microfinance﻿ Institutions﻿ Network(﻿ GHAMFIN).﻿ Apex﻿ banks,﻿
briefly,﻿ are﻿mini﻿ central﻿ banks﻿ in﻿Ghana﻿ that﻿oversee﻿ rural﻿ and﻿community﻿ banks﻿ as﻿well﻿ disburse﻿
funds﻿ to﻿them.﻿ Apex﻿ banks﻿ are﻿financed﻿ through﻿ Ghana’s﻿ Rural﻿ Financial﻿ Services﻿ Project(﻿ RFSP).﻿
Developed﻿ from﻿ a﻿concept﻿ of﻿rural﻿ banking﻿ first﻿conceived﻿ more﻿ than﻿ 40﻿years﻿ ago,﻿the﻿Ministry﻿ of﻿
Finance﻿ and﻿the﻿Bank﻿ of﻿Ghana﻿ have﻿ joined﻿ forces﻿ to﻿attempt﻿ to﻿bring﻿ financial﻿ inclusion﻿ to﻿the﻿rural﻿
population﻿in﻿Ghana﻿in﻿the﻿form﻿of﻿Apex﻿banking﻿systems(﻿Annim,﻿2011,﻿ARB﻿Apex﻿Bank,﻿2013).
Another﻿ important﻿ consideration﻿ in﻿the﻿literature﻿ that﻿is﻿linked﻿ to﻿microfinance﻿ in﻿Ghana﻿ is﻿
demographic﻿ and﻿regulatory﻿ issues﻿ about﻿ the﻿loans.﻿ Aryeetey﻿ and﻿Ahene(﻿ 2005)﻿ conducted﻿ a﻿study﻿
of﻿the﻿effect﻿ of﻿the﻿regulatory﻿ environment﻿ on﻿SMEs﻿ in﻿Ghana.﻿ A﻿summary﻿ of﻿their﻿ demographic﻿
findings﻿that﻿was﻿reported﻿in﻿an﻿earlier﻿study(﻿Etim﻿et﻿al﻿2014),﻿is﻿shown﻿in﻿Table﻿1.
There﻿ were﻿ some﻿ interesting﻿ demographic﻿ and﻿gender﻿ patterns﻿ in﻿the﻿data﻿ shown﻿ in﻿Table﻿ 1:﻿
About﻿ 76%﻿ of﻿businesses﻿ were﻿ owned﻿ by﻿men﻿ even﻿ though﻿ there﻿ were﻿ more﻿ women﻿ than﻿ men﻿ in﻿
Ghana;﻿ 48%﻿ of﻿business﻿ owners﻿ were﻿ in﻿the﻿30-49﻿ age﻿range;﻿ and﻿65%﻿ of﻿the﻿owners﻿ had﻿at﻿least﻿ a﻿
secondary﻿ education.﻿ Additionally,﻿ 58%﻿of﻿the﻿firms﻿ were﻿ sole-proprietorships﻿ and﻿71%﻿of﻿the﻿firms﻿
could﻿ be﻿classified﻿ as﻿small﻿ medium﻿ enterprises(﻿ SMEs﻿ with﻿ 10﻿or﻿fewer﻿ employees),﻿ with﻿ 54%﻿
having﻿ fewer﻿ than﻿six﻿employees.﻿ In﻿that﻿same﻿ study﻿ by﻿Etim﻿ et﻿al.(﻿2014),﻿ also﻿found﻿ that﻿men﻿took﻿
more﻿ risks﻿ to﻿enter﻿ businesses﻿ with﻿ long-term﻿ gestation﻿ periods﻿ while﻿ women﻿ were﻿ more﻿ often﻿ seen﻿
in﻿businesses﻿ with﻿ shorter﻿ gestation﻿ periods.﻿ The﻿authors﻿ did﻿conclude﻿ that﻿this﻿could﻿ be﻿attributed﻿ to﻿
customs﻿ and﻿culture﻿ dictating﻿ that﻿women﻿ were﻿ expected﻿ to﻿use﻿their﻿ income﻿ mostly﻿ for﻿the﻿upkeep﻿
of﻿the﻿home﻿ and﻿their﻿ children﻿ than﻿ establish﻿ businesses﻿ where﻿ return﻿ on﻿investment(﻿ ROI)﻿ might﻿
take﻿longer﻿to﻿be﻿realized.
2.2. Information and Communication Technology Adoption in Ghana
The﻿International ﻿Telecommunication ﻿Union (﻿ITU, ﻿2016) ﻿informed ﻿that﻿ICT,﻿especially ﻿mobile﻿
technology﻿ is﻿widely﻿ adopted﻿ across﻿ all﻿world﻿ regions﻿ including﻿ those﻿ of﻿Sub-Saharan﻿ Africa.﻿ The﻿
Millennium ﻿Development ﻿Goals (﻿MDG, ﻿2000 ﻿–﻿2015) ﻿and﻿the﻿Sustainable ﻿Development ﻿Goals﻿
(SDG,﻿ 2015﻿–﻿ 2030)﻿ have﻿ provided﻿ direction﻿ for﻿global﻿ societies﻿ to﻿build﻿ capacity﻿ using﻿ ICT.﻿ There﻿
is﻿capacity﻿ development﻿ in﻿Ghana﻿ through﻿ mobile﻿ phone﻿ adoption﻿ and﻿use﻿for﻿voice﻿ communication,﻿
text﻿messaging,﻿ internet﻿ resources’﻿ access,﻿ myriad﻿ of﻿mobile﻿ apps﻿ use,﻿mobile﻿ banking﻿ and﻿mobile﻿
money,﻿ uses﻿ in﻿agriculture﻿ and﻿mining,﻿ and﻿mobile﻿ health(﻿ Etim,﻿ 2013;﻿ Imran﻿ and﻿Quimno,﻿ 2016;﻿
Yim﻿&﻿ Gomez,﻿ 2018).﻿ As﻿a﻿result﻿ of﻿mobile﻿ phones﻿ proliferation,﻿ writers﻿ like﻿Imran﻿ and﻿Quimno﻿
(2016) ﻿have﻿started ﻿discussing ﻿mobile ﻿computing ﻿research ﻿platform ﻿that﻿can﻿benefit ﻿many ﻿developing﻿
world﻿ regions.﻿ Mensah(﻿ 2017)﻿ confirmed﻿ widespread﻿ adoption﻿ of﻿mobile﻿ money﻿ such﻿ as﻿electronic﻿
funds﻿ transfers﻿ and﻿mobile﻿ banking﻿ in﻿Ghana.﻿ Owusu(﻿ 2018),﻿ however﻿ argues﻿ that﻿it﻿is﻿important﻿ to﻿
understand﻿ the﻿impact﻿ of﻿social﻿ structure﻿ on﻿mobile﻿ phone﻿ adoption﻿ in﻿Ghana,﻿ its﻿use﻿and﻿impact﻿
through ﻿well﻿develop ﻿ICT﻿policies ﻿that﻿guide ﻿each﻿service ﻿area.﻿For﻿example, ﻿in﻿agriculture, ﻿agricultural﻿
ICT﻿policies’ ﻿formulation ﻿and﻿implementation ﻿would ﻿help﻿to﻿guide ﻿agricultural ﻿markets’ ﻿use﻿of﻿various﻿
ICTs﻿for﻿market﻿transactions.
3. METHODOLOG y
The﻿researchers﻿ used﻿ a﻿survey﻿ to﻿collect﻿ a﻿variety﻿ of﻿information﻿ on﻿loan﻿programs,﻿ micro-loans﻿ and﻿
ICT﻿use﻿among﻿ recipients﻿ of﻿small﻿ businesses﻿ and﻿micro-loan﻿ borrowers﻿ in﻿Ghana.﻿ A﻿faculty﻿ and﻿
students﻿ from﻿ the﻿University﻿ of﻿Cape﻿ Coast,﻿ Ghana﻿ administered﻿ the﻿survey﻿ to﻿small﻿ business﻿ owners﻿
throughout﻿ the﻿four﻿regions﻿ of﻿the﻿country﻿-﻿ Greater﻿ Accra,﻿ Central,﻿ Eastern﻿ and﻿Western﻿ regions﻿International Journal of ICT Research in Africa and the Middle East
Volume 8 • Issue 1 • January-June 2019
4respectively.﻿ The﻿survey﻿ included﻿ questions﻿ profile﻿ information﻿ about﻿ the﻿borrowers,﻿ the﻿cost﻿of﻿
borrowed﻿ funds,﻿ participants’﻿ assessment﻿ of﻿Ghana’s﻿ Entrepreneurship﻿ Program,﻿ their﻿ use﻿of﻿mobile﻿
technologies,﻿and﻿their﻿expectation﻿about﻿the﻿growth﻿of﻿their﻿businesses.
A﻿total﻿ of﻿398﻿survey﻿ responses﻿ were﻿ collected.﻿ The﻿demographic﻿ question﻿ regarding﻿ gender﻿
was﻿used﻿ to﻿segment﻿ the﻿respondents.﻿ Two﻿ respondents﻿ did﻿not﻿provide﻿ their﻿ gender,﻿ leaving﻿ a﻿final﻿
sample﻿size﻿of﻿396.
The﻿data﻿was﻿analyzed ﻿in﻿an﻿attempt ﻿to﻿identify ﻿relationships ﻿between ﻿gender ﻿and﻿various﻿
technology-related﻿ items﻿ on﻿the﻿questionnaire.﻿ Analyses﻿ were﻿ performed﻿ using﻿ chi-square(﻿ X2)﻿tests﻿
of﻿independence﻿ or﻿t-test﻿ on﻿differences﻿ in﻿mean﻿ where﻿ appropriate.﻿ The﻿null﻿hypothesis﻿ in﻿each﻿ test﻿
is﻿that﻿gender﻿ and﻿the﻿variable﻿ being﻿ analyzed﻿ are﻿independent.﻿ The﻿alternative﻿ hypothesis﻿ is﻿that﻿the﻿
two﻿variables﻿are﻿related.
Some ﻿respondents ﻿did﻿not﻿answer ﻿all﻿the﻿technology-related ﻿questions. ﻿If﻿a﻿questionnaire ﻿contained﻿
a﻿valid﻿ response﻿ to﻿a﻿specific﻿ technology﻿ item,﻿ that﻿response﻿ was﻿included﻿ in﻿that﻿item’s﻿ analysis.﻿
Individual﻿ respondent’s﻿ items﻿ that﻿did﻿not﻿contain﻿ answers﻿ on﻿technology﻿ questions﻿ were﻿ excluded﻿
from﻿ that﻿item’s﻿ analysis.﻿ This﻿ means﻿ that﻿a﻿particular﻿ respondent﻿ may﻿ have﻿ been﻿ included﻿ in﻿the﻿
analysis ﻿of﻿one﻿technology ﻿variable ﻿but﻿excluded ﻿from﻿the﻿analysis ﻿of﻿a﻿subsequent ﻿technology ﻿variable.Table 1. Characteristics of firms by age, gender and educational levels of firm owners in the regions of Ghana

4.1. By Region

4.2. By Age
Table ﻿3﻿shows ﻿the﻿distribution ﻿of﻿respondent ﻿age﻿by﻿gender. ﻿The﻿majority ﻿of﻿respondents ﻿were﻿between﻿
26﻿and﻿45﻿years﻿ old.﻿The﻿average﻿ categorical﻿ age﻿for﻿Females﻿ is﻿2.77.﻿ The﻿average﻿ categorical﻿ age﻿
for﻿Males﻿ is﻿2.22.﻿ The﻿difference﻿ in﻿average﻿ age﻿category﻿ is﻿statistically﻿ significant(﻿ p<.001),﻿ with﻿
the﻿Females﻿ being﻿ generally﻿ older.﻿ This﻿ difference﻿ in﻿ages﻿ is﻿confirmed﻿ by﻿the﻿chi-square﻿ difference﻿
test(﻿X2﻿4﻿d.f.﻿=﻿16.56,﻿ p<.01).﻿ The﻿proportion﻿ of﻿Males﻿ in﻿the﻿first﻿age﻿category(﻿ 18-25﻿ years﻿ old)﻿is﻿
significantly﻿ higher﻿ than﻿ the﻿proportion﻿ of﻿Females(﻿ p<.01),﻿ while﻿ the﻿proportion﻿ of﻿Females﻿ in﻿the﻿
fourth﻿age﻿group(﻿46-55﻿years﻿old)﻿is﻿significantly﻿higher﻿than﻿Males(﻿p<.05).
4.3. By Education
Table﻿ 4﻿shows﻿ the﻿distribution﻿ of﻿respondent﻿ education﻿ level﻿ by﻿gender.﻿ The﻿majority﻿ of﻿respondents﻿
have﻿at﻿least﻿a﻿secondary ﻿education. ﻿A﻿significant ﻿difference ﻿exists ﻿between ﻿Females ﻿and﻿Males ﻿across﻿
education ﻿categories (﻿X2﻿5﻿d.f.﻿=﻿20.87, ﻿p<.001), ﻿indicating ﻿a﻿relationship ﻿between ﻿level﻿of﻿education ﻿and﻿
gender.﻿ The﻿proportions﻿ of﻿Males﻿ with﻿ Bachelor’s﻿ and﻿Two-year﻿ College﻿ is﻿marginally﻿ significantly﻿
higher﻿ than﻿ Females(﻿ p<.10).﻿ The﻿proportion﻿ of﻿more﻿ highly﻿ educated﻿ Males(﻿ secondary﻿ schooling﻿
and﻿above)﻿is﻿significantly﻿higher﻿than﻿the﻿proportion﻿of﻿highly﻿educated﻿Females(﻿p<.01).

4.5.3. Mobile Phone Ease of Use
The﻿business﻿ owners﻿ were﻿ asked﻿ to﻿respond﻿ to﻿two﻿statements﻿ on﻿their﻿ perceptions﻿ regarding﻿ the﻿ease﻿
of﻿use﻿of﻿their﻿ mobile﻿ phones.﻿ Both﻿ questions﻿ were﻿ measured﻿ on﻿5-point﻿ Likert-type﻿ scale﻿ ranging﻿
from“﻿Strongly ﻿Disagree” ﻿to“﻿Strongly ﻿Agree.” ﻿The﻿results ﻿from﻿both﻿questions ﻿are﻿shown ﻿in﻿Table ﻿10.
The﻿first﻿statement﻿ was“﻿It﻿is﻿easy﻿ to﻿use﻿my﻿mobile﻿ phone﻿ for﻿voice﻿ communications.”﻿ 235﻿
Females﻿ and﻿146﻿Males﻿ provided﻿ answers.﻿ The﻿mean﻿ score﻿ for﻿was﻿4.10﻿ for﻿Females﻿ and﻿4.29﻿ for﻿
Males﻿ indicating﻿ that﻿both﻿ genders﻿ generally﻿ find﻿the﻿mobile﻿ phone﻿ easy﻿ to﻿use﻿for﻿this﻿purpose.﻿ A﻿
t-test﻿ of﻿mean﻿ scores﻿ indicates﻿ that﻿Males﻿ find﻿their﻿ mobile﻿ phone﻿ marginally﻿ significantly﻿ easier﻿ to﻿
use﻿for﻿voice﻿communication﻿than﻿do﻿Females(﻿p<.10).
The﻿second﻿ statement﻿ was“﻿It﻿is﻿easy﻿ to﻿use﻿my﻿mobile﻿ phone﻿ to﻿make﻿ payments.”﻿ 237﻿Females﻿
and﻿146﻿Males﻿ provided﻿ answers.﻿ The﻿mean﻿ score﻿ for﻿was﻿1.97﻿ for﻿Females﻿ and﻿2.25﻿ for﻿Males,﻿
indicating﻿ that﻿neither﻿ gender﻿ finds﻿ the﻿mobile﻿ phone﻿ easy﻿ to﻿use﻿for﻿payment﻿ purposes.﻿ A﻿t-test﻿
of﻿mean﻿ scores﻿ indicates﻿ that﻿Females﻿ find﻿their﻿ cell﻿phone﻿ significantly﻿ harder﻿ to﻿use﻿for﻿making﻿
payments﻿than﻿do﻿Males(﻿p<.001).
4.5.3. Importance of Mobile Phone for Business
The﻿business ﻿owners ﻿were﻿asked ﻿to﻿respond ﻿to﻿a﻿statement ﻿on﻿their﻿perception ﻿regarding ﻿the﻿importance﻿
of﻿their﻿ mobile﻿ phone﻿ to﻿their﻿ business.﻿ The﻿question﻿ was﻿measured﻿ on﻿5-point﻿ Likert-type﻿ scale﻿
ranging﻿from“﻿Strongly﻿Disagree”﻿to“﻿Strongly﻿Agree.”﻿The﻿results﻿are﻿shown﻿in﻿Table﻿11.
The﻿statement ﻿was“﻿I﻿cannot ﻿run﻿by﻿business ﻿effectively ﻿without ﻿my﻿mobile ﻿phone.” ﻿245﻿Females﻿
and﻿146﻿Males﻿ provided﻿ answers.﻿ The﻿mean﻿ score﻿ for﻿was﻿3.24﻿ for﻿Females﻿ and﻿3.88﻿ for﻿Males﻿
indicating﻿ that﻿both﻿ genders﻿ see﻿their﻿ mobile﻿ phones﻿ adding﻿ moderate﻿ value﻿ to﻿their﻿ businesses.﻿ A﻿
t-test﻿ of﻿mean﻿ scores﻿ indicates﻿ that﻿Males﻿ see﻿significantly﻿ more﻿ business﻿ value﻿ added﻿ from﻿ the﻿use﻿
of﻿their﻿mobile﻿phones﻿than﻿do﻿Females(﻿p<.001).
This﻿ finding﻿ is﻿supported﻿ by﻿a﻿test﻿of﻿proportions.﻿ 133﻿of﻿245﻿Females﻿ respondents(﻿ 54.3%)﻿
“Agree” ﻿or“﻿Strongly ﻿Agree” ﻿with﻿the﻿statement, ﻿while ﻿108﻿of﻿146﻿Males (﻿74.0%) “﻿Agree” ﻿or﻿
“Strongly﻿ Agree”.﻿ The﻿proportion﻿ of﻿Males﻿ with﻿ positive﻿ responses﻿ is﻿significantly﻿ higher﻿ than﻿ the﻿
Females(﻿p<.01).
